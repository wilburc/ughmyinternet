Analyzing/Visualizing Messenger Data

Us card-holding Millennials™ are no strangers to the cultural phenomenon known as the “group chat.” Overwhelming social anxiety combined with unwillingness to go outside and the pressure to maintain some form of social interaction result in these chatrooms effectively replacing real life conversation.

The most meme-worthy and viral offspring of “the group chat” is, of course,

when the group chat lit 🔥🔥🔥

But no one ever asks…

when the group chat lit 🔥🔥🔥?

The “lit-ness” of a group chat is generally represented by furious typing, suggesting a high volume of messages being sent in the chat. For more memes described, feel free to email me.

I took the data from my most active group chat (which, funnily enough, is actually just 3 people, one of whom rarely shows up) and plotted the daily message count to see if there was anything interesting. Note that it takes some work to get the data into the form we want. Luckily, I’ve included my python notebook here.

Number of messages per day. November 2017 day wasn’t included in my FB data download for some reason. Code

Ignoring the lack of November 2017 data (not sure what happened there, Zuck), it’s… pretty lit if I say so myself. The numbers are consistently high, especially considering the fact that this group chat is really just 2 active people. We sure know how to have a good time.

Apart from looking into which catastrophic life-events corresponded to the 3 most active days, there isn’t much to do with this. Knowing that the weekends probably account for the sharp drops in messages, each week seems more or less the same.

messages
Various metrics for number of messages. Code

At the daily level, every weekday is generally around the same, with a slight downward trend as the week progresses— and it’s clear that weekends just aren’t great. This makes sense, as the group chat in question was originally created for workplace comiseration.

Hourly, 10am-noon is clearly “when the group chat is lit 🔥.” This might seem unusual at first, but the morning is when we’re most wired on caffeine and typing at top speeds. It would be interesting to see if this trend dropped when I cut out coffee a few months ago. The next notable trend is the sharp drop every day around 1pm—I mean… I can’t eat my Panda Express and chat at the same time!

While the graphs don’t necessarily uncover any groundbreaking insights, it’s fun to see how daily habits and routines are reflected in something as trivial as a group chat.
